Discover Life

Where life might exist — and what it needs to begin.

Mars

Mars once had rivers, lakes, and possibly oceans. Today, most water is locked as ice beneath the surface. Its core is cooling, but ancient conditions may have supported microbial life. Scientists search for organic molecules and signs of past habitability.

Europa

Europa has a global subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust. Tidal heating from Jupiter keeps the ocean warm enough for liquid water. This makes Europa one of the strongest candidates for extraterrestrial life in our solar system.

Enceladus

Enceladus has a subsurface ocean and active hydrothermal vents. Water jets erupt from cracks in the ice, carrying organic molecules from the ocean below. This moon is one of the most promising places to search for life today.

Titan

Titan has a thick atmosphere, methane lakes, and a subsurface ocean. Its chemistry is complex and resembles early Earth. Life here might be very different from life on Earth, possibly using methane instead of water.

Exoplanets

Thousands of exoplanets have been discovered. Some orbit in the habitable zone, where temperatures allow liquid water. Scientists study their atmospheres, magnetic fields, and geology to determine whether they could support life.

Building Blocks of Life

Cells are the basic units of life. On other worlds, scientists look for structures that can grow, reproduce, and use energy.

DNA stores genetic information. Life elsewhere might use different molecules, but we search for any system that can store and pass on information.

Biomolecules such as proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates are essential for structure and energy. We search for organic molecules on planets and moons.

Energy is required for life. Planets with sunlight, chemical reactions, or hydrothermal vents are strong candidates for habitability.
