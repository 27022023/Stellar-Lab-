Planetary Systems

How planets form, evolve, and shape the architecture of their cosmic neighborhoods.

How Planetary Systems Form

Planetary systems begin inside vast clouds of gas and dust. When gravity causes these clouds to collapse, they form a rotating disk called a protoplanetary disk. Within this disk, dust grains collide and stick together, slowly growing into larger bodies called planetesimals.

Over millions of years, these planetesimals merge into full-sized planets. Their final arrangement depends on many factors: the mass of the star, the temperature of the disk, and the chaotic gravitational interactions between forming worlds.

Types of Planets

Planetary systems often contain a mix of rocky planets, gas giants, ice giants, and exotic worlds unlike anything in our Solar System. Many exoplanets orbit extremely close to their stars, forming “hot Jupiters,” while others orbit in wide, cold regions far from stellar warmth.

Habitability

Astrobiology focuses on the habitable zone — the region around a star where liquid water can exist. But habitability is more complex than distance alone. Planetary atmospheres, magnetic fields, geology, and chemistry all play crucial roles in determining whether a world can support life.
